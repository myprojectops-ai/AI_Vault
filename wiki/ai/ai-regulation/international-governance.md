# International AI Governance

Efforts to coordinate AI governance across borders — and why it's so hard.

## Key Forums and Summits

### AI Safety Summits
- **Bletchley Park** (November 2023): 28 countries signed the Bletchley Declaration
  acknowledging frontier AI risks
- **Seoul** (May 2024): Follow-up with voluntary safety commitments from labs
- **Paris** (February 2025): Broader participation; focus on inclusive governance
- Established a pattern of high-level political engagement but limited binding
  commitments

### International AI Safety Reports
- **2026 report:** 30+ countries, 100+ experts
- Key finding: reliable safety testing has become harder as models distinguish
  test environments from deployment
- Warns that risk management frameworks are immature globally

### G7 and G20
- Hiroshima AI Process (G7): Voluntary code of conduct for advanced AI
- G20 discussions on AI governance principles
- Both focused on interoperability between national frameworks

## Why International Governance Is Hard

1. **Speed mismatch:** AI develops in months; treaties take years
2. **Verification challenge:** Unlike nuclear weapons, AI capabilities are hard
   to measure and monitor externally
3. **Dual-use nature:** The same model is useful for healthcare and bioweapons
4. **Commercial interests:** Companies lobby against binding restrictions
5. **Geopolitical competition:** US and China can't agree on AI governance while
   racing for AI supremacy
6. **Technical complexity:** Most policymakers don't understand the technology
   well enough to regulate it effectively

## AI Arms Control Prospects

In the AI 2027 scenario:
- Diplomats consider what "AI arms control" might look like
- Compute-based monitoring is theoretically possible (datacenters are hard to hide)
- Nuclear arms control provides a starting template but AI has new challenges
- Treaties are "viewed less favorably than attempts at unilaterally increasing
  America's lead"
- China has natural interest in arms control from a position of weakness, but
  overtures lead nowhere

Real world:
- No serious bilateral AI arms control negotiations exist
- Compute-based governance proposals exist in academic literature but no political
  traction
- The gap between academic proposals and political reality is vast

## Emerging Frameworks

- **EU AI Act** as a potential global template (see [[eu-ai-act]])
- **OECD AI Principles** adopted by 40+ countries
- **UNESCO Recommendation on AI Ethics** (2021)
- Various bilateral technology agreements
- None of these are binding on the frontier labs that matter most

## Key Takeaways

- International AI governance exists on paper but lacks enforcement mechanisms
- The speed of AI development outpaces the speed of international coordination
- US-China competition is the single biggest obstacle to meaningful cooperation
- Compute-based monitoring is the most promising technical approach but politically
  stalled
- The AI 2027 scenario's depiction of failed arms control attempts is realistic
