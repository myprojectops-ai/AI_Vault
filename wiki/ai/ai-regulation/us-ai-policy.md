# US AI Policy

The US approach to AI governance — lighter touch than the EU, with a focus
on competitiveness and national security over prescriptive regulation.

## Key Policy Actions

### Biden Era (2023-2024)
- **Executive Order 14110** (October 2023): Required safety testing for powerful AI,
  established AI Safety Institute (AISI), mandated reporting of large training runs
- Created a framework-based approach rather than hard law
- Established voluntary commitments from major AI companies

### Trump Era (2025+)
- Rescinded Biden's AI executive order early in term
- Focus shifted to AI competitiveness and deregulation
- December 2025: Approved conditional Nvidia H200 exports to China
- In AI 2027 scenario: "The President defers to his advisors, tech industry leaders
  who argue that nationalization would 'kill the goose that lays the golden eggs'"

## US AI Safety Institute (AISI)

- Established within NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology)
- Mission: evaluate frontier models before deployment
- In practice: limited staff and authority compared to the scale of the problem
- In AI 2027: OpenBrain presents models to AISI but exploits narrow definition
  of "deployment" to avoid oversight of internal use
- AISI's future uncertain under current administration

## Congressional Action

- Multiple AI bills introduced but legislative progress slow
- Key proposals: mandatory safety testing, watermarking requirements, liability
  frameworks
- Bipartisan concern about AI but partisan disagreement on solutions
- In AI 2027: "Congress fires off subpoenas" after the misalignment memo leak,
  but by then the government is playing catch-up

## Defense and Intelligence

- DOD increasing AI integration for cyber, data analysis, and R&D
- DARPA funding advanced AI capabilities research
- Intelligence community concerned about AI-enabled espionage and influence operations
- In AI 2027: DOD's integration is "slow due to bureaucracy and procurement process"

## State-Level Action

- California has been most active (SB-1047 AI safety bill debated in 2024)
- Other states pursuing their own AI regulations
- Patchwork of state laws creates compliance complexity

## The Core Tension

The US faces a fundamental conflict:
- **Competitiveness:** Aggressive AI development to maintain lead over China
- **Safety:** Precautionary regulation to prevent harm
- **Current resolution:** Competitiveness wins; regulation is light and voluntary
- **Risk:** If something goes wrong, the response will be reactive, not preventive

## Key Takeaways

- US AI policy prioritizes competitiveness over precautionary regulation
- The regulatory environment is lighter than the EU, with more voluntary frameworks
- AISI exists but lacks the authority and resources for meaningful oversight
- The AI 2027 scenario's depiction of government playing catch-up rings true
- The US approach bets on being able to fix problems faster than they emerge
